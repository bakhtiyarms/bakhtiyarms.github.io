Climate Conversations: Visualising Inclusivity Through COP Participation and Representation

Bakhtiyar's Project Page

Aim of the Project

One of the key missions of the United Nations Climate Change Conferences (COPs) is to achieve inclusive climate action worldwide. Therefore, the main aim of this project is to analyse the inclusivity of COP events over time by constructing various graphs and visualisations. Inclusivity is assessed based on such metrics as participant diversity, regional representation distribution, and the involvement of non-governmental organisations (NGOs). Through an analysis of attendance trends, participant categories, regional hosting dynamics, observer NGO involvement, and global CO₂ emissions, this report aims to shed light on the evolution of COP events, which significant resources are spent on, and their role in fostering inclusive global dialogue on climate action.

* The term “party” or “parties” refer to the states that are both parties to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change and represented at the COP.

How many people attend the COPs?

The attendance has significantly increased over the period. While the first COP hosted approximately only 3600 attendees, this number exceeded 70,000 in COP29.

I obtained the data from UNFCCC and manually constructed a CSV file.

How does hosting the COP influence the hosting countries' renewable energy usage?

It is observed that host nations' renewable energy usage fluctuates differently.

I obtained the data of all the world economies for the past half century on renewable energy output from the World Bank and scraped it on Colab to get the data on the COP-hosting countries between the first COP in 1995 and Paris Agreement in 2015.

Who attends the COPs most?

While the largest category in COP29 was Parties with 17,680 participants, COP28 was attended the most by Overflow with 24,890 participants. NGO and media participation demonstrated a broad, diverse range of participants over the last period.

I obtained the data from UNFCCC and Carbon Brief as well as national COP presidencynews portals and manually constructed a CSV file.

How are the regions represented globally?

The "Western Europe and Other Group" hosted the most conferences with 12 COPs attended by around 320,000 participants. However, the total attendance almost reached half a million in COP28 and COP29 which were held in the "Asia & Pacific Group" and "Eastern Europe Group", respectively.

I obtained the data from UNFCCC and manually constructed a CSV file.

How has NGO involvement in the COPs evolved?

The trend exhibits a gradual increase over time, reaching over 500 NGOs in COP26 and COP28, indicating a growing NGO involvement in climate negotiations in recent years.

I obtained the data from UNFCCC and Carbon Brief and manually constructed a CSV file.

Has the COPs decreased global CO₂ emissions?

Emissions have steadily increased over time, surpassing 35 billion tonnes by 2024. Despite the first COP in 1995 (red line) and the Paris Agreement, where the Parties agreed to keep the global annual temperature rise below 1.5 ºC, in 2015 (blue line), global emissions continued to rise, indicating that international agreements have not yet managed to slow the down the global carbon emissions.

I obtained the CSV file of the data from the World Bank, cleaned it, and constructed the chart on Vega editor.

How do the wealthy economies pollute the environment?

The G7 countries are the wealthiest economies in the world; therefore, their contribution to climate finance is of paramount importance. They are supposed to compensate for the amount of emissions they produce.

I obtained the data of major economies on greenhouse gas emissions from the World Bank and scraped it on Colab to get the data on G7 countries.

The Data

The majority of data comes from the UNFCCC sources that collects and presents the data and visualisations in a publicly accessible format. It is also possible to download CSV files for most of the visualisations presented on the UNFCCC website. It helped me obtain the primary data later which I cleaned and adjusted for my own charts.

The next data source is the official websites of each individual COP events, such as cop28.com and cop29.az. These websites were particularly useful to delve into more detailed statistics and gather particular information on the individual COP trends.

Another data source is Carbon Brief, which is a UK-based website covering the latest developments in climate science, climate policy and energy policy. Since it specialises in clear, data-driven articles, it helped me improve the understanding of climate change, both in terms of the science and the policy response.

I also used the World Bank and International Energy Agency (IEA) databases to obtain the data on global carbon emissions and renewable energy usage.

Analytical Challenges

For this analysis, it was not possible to create charts that automatically update because there were no particular API sources in this topic. While the topic concerns an important issue – reaching inclusivity in climate action – worldwide, the main data sources and databases belong to the UNFCCC and individual COP hosting states that do not produce APIs. Although it was possible to find some API sources for particular parties, such as in the case of the UK, it was not feasible to present a whole analysis of the UNFCCC and COP parties as a whole. Therefore, I mainly used CSV files for my data analysis.

AAnother challenge was related to categorical overlap in participating categories. The main aim of this project was to analyse the inclusivity in COP events and, therefore, the participation of NGOs was the primary target of analysis. However, some participants belonged to multiple categories which complicated the classification of participant categories. For instance, academic affiliations were shown both in academic and NGO classification in some contexts.

Conclusion

The project summarises the complex relationship between inclusivity in climate transition and the global response to climate change. It reveals a notable increase in NGO involvement over the years as observer organisations to the UNFCCC process, which voices the increased efforts to address diverse and marginalised groups in the global climate dialogue. Nonetheless, the constant increase in annual CO₂ emissions worldwide alarms us of an urgent risk of a failure to cultivate inclusive representation with tangible climate outcomes.

While inclusivity is one of the key missions of the COPs and global climate conversation in order to protect the Indigenous and marginalised populations worldwide from climate threats, these efforts have to be coupled with tangible commitments and practical implementation so that the consequences of climate change do not endanger life in the world. As a result, these visualisations call on humanity to work harder in order to address the risks of climate change and achieve global justice where no resources are wasted and no life is threatened.
